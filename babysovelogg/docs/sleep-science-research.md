# Baby Sleep Science: Research Summary

Research compiled 2026-03-30. References linked where available.

## 1. Circadian Rhythm Development (0-3 years)

### Timeline

| Age | Milestone |
|-----|-----------|
| Prenatal | Fetal SCN (suprachiasmatic nucleus) begins developing at ~40% gestation. Melatonin receptors present at 18 weeks gestation. Fetus relies entirely on maternal circadian signals. |
| Birth | No endogenous melatonin production. Sleep distributed roughly equally across 24 hours. ~70% of time spent sleeping (16-17h). |
| 2 weeks | Sleep in ~4h intervals. Fetal circadian rhythms fading. |
| 5-6 weeks | Emergence of infradian rhythm (~25h period). Endogenous melatonin first becomes detectable. |
| 9-12 weeks | Melatonin day-night rhythm begins. Cortisol circadian rhythm emerging (2-9 months range). |
| 3-4 months | Circadian rhythm entrained to 24h cycle. Melatonin production stable. Diurnal sleep-wake pattern established. |
| 15 weeks | More consolidated wake/sleep episodes apparent. |
| 6-9 months | Most infants can produce 6+ hour consolidated sleep episodes at night. |
| 1-3 years | SCN reaches adult neuron levels. |

### Key Hormones

**Melatonin**: Newborns cannot produce it. Breastfed infants receive maternal melatonin through breast milk (~35% of mother's blood serum concentration). Endogenous production begins ~6 weeks, with a distinct day-night rhythm emerging at 9-12 weeks.

**Cortisol**: Circadian cortisol rhythm observable from 2 weeks to 9 months. Promotes wakefulness. Also transmitted through breast milk with diurnal variation.

**Breast milk composition varies by time of day**: Day milk has higher cortisol, tyrosine, and immune factors. Night milk has elevated leptin, melatonin, and tryptophan. This is a significant entrainment signal.

## 2. Sleep Architecture Development

### Sleep Cycles (Ultradian Rhythm)

- Newborns: ~50-60 min cycles, beginning in Active Sleep (REM)
- By 3 months: sleep spindles appear in NREM stage 2
- 3-12 months: cycle length increases ~10 min
- By 6 months: all sleep should begin with NREM onset (adult-like pattern)
- School age: 85-90 min cycles
- Adults: 90-120 min cycles, 4-6 per night

### REM vs NREM

- Birth: REM (Active Sleep) = 50-80% of total sleep
- 6 months: NREM increasing, REM < 50%
- 12 months: Quiet sleep 35-50% of total
- Adults: REM = ~25%

### EEG Milestones
- Birth: trace alternant present (disappears by 6 weeks)
- 3 months: sleep spindles appear
- First months: slow-wave sleep emerges

### References
- [The Architecture of Early Childhood Sleep Over the First Two Years (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9925493/)
- [Developmental Changes in Ultradian Sleep Cycles (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5584621/)

| 24 months | ~13h | | | 1 |

Note: Data from multiple studies; exact numbers vary by study and methodology. High inter-individual variability reported consistently.

### Night Wakings
- 0-2 months: 0-3.4 episodes per night
- 6 months: ~50% have 1-2 awakenings; 21% have >2
- 9 months: 26% have >2 awakenings
- 1-2 years: 0-2.5 per night
- Night wakings remain relatively stable through the first year

### Nap Transitions
- 3 to 2 naps: typically 6.5-9 months (most common 7-8 months)
- 2 to 1 nap: typically 13-18 months (12 months usually too early)

## 4. Wake Windows: What Does Science Actually Say?

### The Evidence Base

**Important caveat**: There is no direct clinical research that tests "wake windows" as a concept. The idea is derived indirectly from:
- Average sleep totals for different age groups
- Research on effects of overtiredness/sleep deprivation
- Homeostatic sleep pressure (adenosine buildup) research
- Clinical experience of pediatric sleep consultants

### The Two-Process Model (Borbely)

Wake windows are best understood through the two-process model of sleep regulation:

**Process S (Homeostatic)**: Sleep pressure builds during wakefulness via adenosine accumulation. Adenosine builds particularly quickly in infants because they use enormous amounts of energy for growth and learning, hitting their "sleep pressure threshold" sooner than adults.

**Process C (Circadian)**: The internal clock promotes wakefulness during the day and sleep at night. In infants, this system is initially uncoordinated with Process S, causing fragmented newborn sleep. As the circadian rhythm matures, these two systems align.

### Commonly Cited Wake Windows by Age

| Age | Wake window |
|-----|------------|
| 0-4 weeks | 30-60 min |
| 4-12 weeks | 60-90 min |
| 3-4 months | 75-120 min |
| 5-7 months | 2-3 hours |
| 7-10 months | 2.5-3.5 hours |
| 11-14 months | 3-4 hours |
| 14-24 months | 4-6 hours |
| 2+ years | 6+ hours |

### Criticism

These are population averages, not individually validated thresholds. Every baby is different, and sleep needs vary. The concept is useful as a rough guideline but should not be treated as precise.

## 5. Predictability of Baby Sleep

### How Predictable Is It?

**High inter-individual variability** is the consistent finding across studies. Changes in infant sleep duration follow different nonlinear trajectories -- babies start with different baseline sleep amounts and do not follow the same developmental paths.

### What IS Predictable

At a population level, general trends are reliable:
- Nighttime sleep increases over first year
- Daytime sleep decreases
- Sleep consolidates
- Nap count decreases

One longitudinal study (N=52) achieved surprisingly high prediction accuracy:
- **94.2% accuracy** at predicting sleep status at 6 months from 1-month variables
- **84.6% accuracy** at predicting 12-month status from 1-month variables

### What IS NOT Predictable

For an individual baby on a given day, precision is much lower. Baby sleep patterns:
- Vary from individual to individual
- Vary across cultures
- Don't follow one universal schedule
- Are affected by many transient factors (illness, teething, milestones, travel)

### Best Predictive Variables (from longitudinal research)

**Variables at 1 month that predicted sleep status at 6 and 12 months:**
1. Parental presence at sleep onset (strongest predictor -- associated with poorer self-regulation)
2. Frequency of infant night waking
3. Total infant sleep time

**Variables predicting self-soothing at 12 months:**
1. Decreasing time spent out of crib across first year
2. High levels of quiet sleep at birth
3. Longer parental response times to awakenings at 3 months

**NOT predictive** (in some studies): feeding method, sleeping arrangements, gender, socioeconomic status.

## 6. Factors Influencing Baby Sleep Timing

### Age
The single strongest factor. Sleep architecture, total sleep need, and wake tolerance all change dramatically with brain maturation. Age determines the baseline around which everything else varies.

### Light Exposure
- Babies who slept well at night were exposed to significantly more light in the early afternoon
- Cycled light/dark exposure (vs continuous light) significantly improves circadian rhythm establishment
- The first few weeks of life may be a "sensitive window of circadian programming"
- Outdoor activity >1 hour/day associated with better sleep patterns

### Feeding
- Breastfed infants receive circadian-varying hormones (melatonin, cortisol) through milk
- Breastfed infants show more regular nocturnal melatonin patterns
- However: infants still breastfed at 12 months more likely to have fragmented sleep (likely due to feeding-to-sleep association, not breastfeeding itself)
- Introduction of solids before 4 months associated with shorter sleep duration

### Prior Sleep (Homeostatic Pressure)
- Duration of prior wakefulness directly determines sleep pressure (adenosine buildup)
- Quality and duration of prior naps affect subsequent wake tolerance
- This is the basis of the "wake window" concept

### Activity and Stimulation
- Screen exposure increases risk of poor sleep patterns
- Outdoor activity associated with better sleep
- Motor milestone acquisition can temporarily disrupt sleep

### Parental Behavior
- Independent sleep initiation associated with longer consolidated sleep
- Parental presence at sleep onset strongest predictor of fragmented sleep at 6 and 12 months
- Number of settling activities at 1 month predicts poorer sleep at 12 months

### References
- [The role of light exposure in infant circadian rhythm establishment (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11685245/)
- [The relationship between daytime light and night-time sleep in 6-12 week infants](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15560769/)
- [Light and maternal influence in entrainment of activity circadian rhythm (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4955618/)

## 7. Sleep Regressions: What Does Research Say?

### The Key Finding

**There is essentially no peer-reviewed research on "sleep regressions."** The term does not appear in the medical literature. Pediatric sleep researcher Jodi Mindell analyzed survey data and concluded: "The data clearly indicate that there's no specific age at which all of a sudden you see a shift in sleep."

### What Actually Happens

Rather than predictable regressions at fixed ages, what parents experience is:
- **Sleep architecture maturation** (especially at ~4 months when sleep transitions from 2-stage newborn pattern to 4-stage mature pattern)
- **Motor milestone disruptions** (rolling, crawling, standing, walking) -- babies practice new skills
- **Nap transition periods** (dropping a nap temporarily destabilizes schedule)
- **Separation anxiety** (~8-10 months, ~18 months)
- **Illness, teething, travel** -- transient disruptions
- **Growth spurts** requiring extra feeding

### The "4-Month Regression"

The most cited regression. This is actually a permanent maturation of sleep architecture: transition from 2-stage (active/quiet) newborn sleep to 4-stage adult-like sleep cycles. Not really a regression -- it is developmental progress, but the lighter sleep stages cause more brief awakenings.

### Duration

These episodes typically resolve within about 1 week without intervention.

### References
- [Are sleep regressions real? Examining the evidence (Dr. Craig Canapari)](https://drcraigcanapari.com/are-sleep-regressions-real/)
- [Sleep Regressions (Sleep Foundation)](https://www.sleepfoundation.org/baby-sleep/4-month-sleep-regression)

## 8. Published Prediction Models and Algorithms

### Academic Research

**Predicting Infant Sleep from Maternal Mental Health (2026)**
- Six ML algorithms tested (logistic regression, random forest, SVC, XGBoost, LightGBM, MLP)
- Best model: precision-recall AUC 0.92, F1 0.84, accuracy 0.88 for predicting short nocturnal sleep
- Used maternal depression, anxiety, PTSD symptoms as predictors
- [JMIR Pediatrics](https://pediatrics.jmir.org/2026/1/e78937)

**Sleep Well Baby (2022)**
- Real-time sleep staging for preterm infants
- Input: heart rate, respiratory rate, SpO2
- Random forest classifier: AUROC 0.69-0.82 (training), 0.61-0.78 (validation)
- Classifies wake, quiet sleep, active sleep
- [SLEEP journal (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9548667/)

**Longitudinal Sleep Prediction (2020)**
- 1-month variables predicted 6-month sleep status with 94.2% accuracy
- 1-month variables predicted 12-month sleep status with 84.6% accuracy
- Key variables: parental presence at onset, night waking frequency, total sleep time
- [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7667498/)

### Commercial Apps

**Huckleberry (SweetSpot)**
- Uses "hundreds of millions of data points" from tracked sleep
- Starts with age-appropriate wake windows, personalizes from logged data
- Looks at last 5+ days of logged sleep for pattern detection
- Combines AI with pediatric sleep consultant guidance
- No published methodology/peer-reviewed validation
- [Huckleberry SweetSpot description](https://huckleberrycare.com/blog/sweetspot-your-smart-sleep-timing-companion)

**Napper**
- Predicts next nap based on "biological rhythms"
- Needs ~1 week of data for accurate predictions
- Adjusts as sleep needs change
- No published methodology

**Bambii**
- Uses machine learning, adjusts wake windows by age and sleep history
- No published methodology

**None of these apps have published their prediction algorithms or submitted them to peer review.**

## 9. Implications for a Sleep Prediction System

### What the Science Supports Building

Based on this research, a reasonable prediction system could use:

1. **Age as the primary variable** -- determines baseline wake window range and total sleep need
2. **Time since last sleep ended** (homeostatic pressure / Process S) -- the single most actionable input
3. **Time of day** (circadian influence / Process C) -- especially important after 3-4 months when circadian rhythm is established
4. **Prior nap duration and quality** -- affects how much sleep pressure was discharged
5. **Total sleep in prior 24 hours** -- context for overall sleep debt
6. **Historical patterns for this specific child** -- personalization from logged data (Huckleberry approach)
7. **Number of naps already taken today** -- determines which nap slot we're predicting

### What the Science Says Is Hard/Impossible to Predict

- Exact minute a baby will fall asleep (too many variables)
- Impact of one-off disruptions (illness, teething, milestones)
- Individual baby's deviation from population averages without tracking data
- Night waking count on a given night

### Honest Accuracy Expectations

- Population-level nap timing windows: reasonable (within 30-60 min)
- Individual baby with 1+ week of data: better (within 15-30 min for regular babies)
- Perfect prediction: not achievable -- infant sleep has inherent stochasticity
- The Huckleberry approach (5+ days of data, age-based priors, pattern matching) is about as good as commercial systems get, and even they show predictions as ranges, not exact times

### Core Algorithm Concept

```
predicted_nap_time = last_wake_time + adjusted_wake_window

where:
  base_wake_window = f(age_in_weeks)           # lookup table from research
  personal_adjustment = f(recent_logged_data)    # deviation from population mean
  circadian_modifier = f(time_of_day, nap_number) # later naps may have shorter/longer windows
  sleep_debt_modifier = f(total_sleep_last_24h - expected_total_sleep)

  adjusted_wake_window = base_wake_window + personal_adjustment + circadian_modifier + sleep_debt_modifier
```

This is essentially what Huckleberry and similar apps do, though with more data and more sophisticated pattern matching.

## 12. Nap-Cap / Daily-Total Smoothing — Evidence Base

Added 2026-05-13 to ground the `napBudget` engine feature. The earlier
sections cover cycles and consolidation but not the literature on *short*
naps, on slow-wave-activity (SWA) distribution between nap and night, or
on the cost of waking mid-deep-sleep. Those are the gaps below.

### Short naps restore alertness without a full cycle

The folk-wisdom "wake at a cycle boundary" rule overstates the importance
of completing a full 45–60 min infant cycle. Adult and infant evidence:

- **Brooks & Lack 2006** (*SLEEP* 29:831-840). Compared 5/10/20/30 min nap
  opportunities under sleep restriction. The 10-min nap produced the
  largest, most durable benefits to alertness, vigilance, and cognition,
  with effects visible 155 min post-nap. 20- and 30-min naps were also
  effective but with more sleep inertia. The 5-min nap was useless —
  subjects didn't reach N2. Practical floor for a useful adult nap: ~10
  min of consolidated sleep, i.e. enough to reach N2.

- **Mednick, Nakayama & Stickgold 2003** (*Nat Neurosci* 6:697-698, "A
  nap is as good as a night"). **Stage 2 NREM alone is sufficient for
  measurable cognitive restoration.** N3/SWS is not required for a nap
  to be restorative.

- **Friedrich et al. 2015** (*PNAS*, "Generalization of word meanings
  during infant sleep" / "Timely sleep facilitates declarative memory
  consolidation in infants"). Memory benefits demonstrated in 9- and
  12-month-olds from naps as short as 30 min.

- **Stages reached in a 20-30 min infant nap.** N1 in ~5 min, N2 by
  ~10-15 min, early N3 possibly from ~25-30 min. Adult and infant cycle
  architectures share this pattern (Grigg-Damberger 2016, *J Clin Sleep
  Med* 12:1599). A 20-30 min nap is almost pure N1+N2 — exactly the
  stage profile shown to restore alertness in adults and demonstrated
  to support memory in infants.

**Implication:** A "minimum useful nap" floor should be age-anchored
around 20-25 min, not "learned cycle minus 5". The cycle-boundary
heuristic is a soft preference, not a hard constraint.

### SWA distribution: most slow-wave is night-loaded

- **Achermann & Borbély 1993** (*J Sleep Res*). The two-process model:
  slow-wave activity is highest in the first NREM cycle of the night,
  declines exponentially across the night. ~50-70% of total nightly
  SWA is discharged in the first 2-3 cycles. SWS clusters in the first
  half of the night, REM dominates the second half.

- **Kurth et al. 2016** (*J Sleep Res*, "Development of nap
  neurophysiology"). Followed 2-, 3-, and 5-year-olds. **SWA in naps
  decreases sharply across early childhood** — at 2 yr, naps carry
  substantial SWA; by 5 yr, daytime SWA is greatly reduced. The
  homeostatic build-up of sleep need across the day attenuates as the
  child matures, which is why naps disappear without harm.

- **Lassonde et al. 2016** (*Sleep Health*, "Sleep physiology in
  toddlers: effects of missing a nap on subsequent night sleep").
  **Missing a single nap in 2-year-olds produced a 13% increase in
  nighttime SWA and a 25% bump in sleep efficiency.** Direct evidence
  that missed daytime SWA is rapidly recouped at night.

**Implication:** Capping an infant nap before significant N3 accrues
doesn't create persistent SWA debt — the homeostatic system pushes it
to that night's first cycle, which is where SWA is densest anyway.

### Long naps demonstrably cost night sleep

- **Nakagawa et al. 2016** (*Scientific Reports* 6:27246, "Daytime nap
  controls toddlers' nighttime sleep"). Actigraphy + diary in 50
  toddlers: clean negative correlation between nap duration and night
  sleep — longer naps → later onset and shorter night.

- **Akacem, LeBourgeois et al. 2015** (*PLOS One*). Napping toddlers had
  **69 min less night sleep and 38 min later DLMO** than non-nappers.
  Correlational, but the homeostatic mechanism is solid: a long nap
  discharges Process S that would otherwise drive bedtime sleep onset.

- **Pediatric consultant consensus** (Weissbluth, Mindell, Pantley)
  anchors on bedtime preservation via the nap-to-bedtime gap (≥3-4 h
  wake before bedtime). Not anchored to cycle physiology. The 90-min
  pre-bedtime buffer used in `RESCUE_NAP.MIN_PRE_BEDTIME_WAKE` is in
  this evidence-consistent range.

**Implication:** Capping an over-budget nap to protect bedtime is
evidence-consistent. The cap (driven by total-sleep-budget) is the
real constraint; cycle-boundary math is decoration.

### Sleep inertia from mid-N3 wakes is real but transient

- **Trotti 2017** (*Sleep Med Rev*, "Waking up is the hardest thing I
  do all day"). Forced waking from N3 produces 15-60 min of impaired
  cognition and irritability; N1/N2/REM wakes produce far less.

- The wake-cycle-phase rule is most relevant in the 6 mo – 2 yr window
  when nap SWS is abundant and the infant is least able to
  self-regulate the transition. Less relevant in newborns (sleep less
  stage-differentiated) or after ~2-3 yr (declining nap SWS). An 11mo
  is probably near the worst case for inertia from a badly-timed wake.

**Implication:** Within an already-decided cap window, prefer waking
during a likely lighter-sleep moment (the ±8 min boundary heuristic in
`computeCyclePhase`). But never extend past the cap chasing a perfect
boundary — the cap is the constraint.

### Nap-floor table for engine constants

Reading from the above, the literature-backed minimum useful nap by age:

| Age (months) | Floor (min) | Source |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 4 | 20 | Brooks & Lack adult floor + Mednick N2-restoration; cycle ~45-50 min, N2 reached by ~10-15 min |
| 11 | 22 | Friedrich 2015: memory benefits at 30 min, 12mo; N2 by ~15 min |
| 18 | 28 | Cycle ~50-60 min; meaningful N3 contribution; below ~25 is drowsy time |
| 36 | 30 | Kurth 2016: nap SWA declining but still useful; transition window |

These are *floors*, not targets. Targets at 11mo remain 1-2 h total
daytime sleep per Iglowstein/Galland normative data.

### Where the literature is thin

- Exact SWA discharge curves for infant naps under 2 yr (mostly
  extrapolated from Kurth 2-3-5 yr data and adult Achermann/Borbély).
- No RCT on capping at 30 vs 60 vs 90 min in <18mo (no head-to-head
  trial exists).
- Validity of accelerometer/movement-based cycle estimation in
  infants (consumer smart-alarm validations are all adult).
